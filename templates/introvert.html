Part 1

Step 1: Just decide to show up.
Step 2: Show up.


If you aren't an introvert, your first step will be to choose a group or a specific event to attend. However, in my experience, it doesn't matter what your first couple events are, or if the events don't facilitate future connections. Overcoming my aversion to these unfamiliar social situations required practice.

Practice didn't cure the root of my aversion, but it gave me enough first-hand (yet anecdotal) evidence that the benefits outweigh the costs. On rare occasions, I leave an event feeling that I didn't gain anything by going. Even so, I have yet to regret going because I have come to strongly believe that you can't predict ahead of time which events will be great (but I will still write a post to help you try).

As for the costs, they aren't as big as they seem. Sometimes, I am awkward. A couple weeks ago, I delayed two people's departure from the meetup with a very unnecessary tangent about sheep farming in Iceland. Sometimes, I don't talk to any new people, even though I spend time coaching myself to do just that. The only consequence, other than cringing in retrospect, is that I have to work on new strategies to appear less awkward than I am at heart.


{{cred2}}


If your life doesn't present you with obstacles that provide reasonable excuses to skip an evening of networking, than I am jealous. The likelihood is that if you don't love the idea of networking, your brain will come up with legitimate excuses not to do it. If your brain is stubborn, then the excuses will be (or seem) very good. My only advice is this: go anyways. Set goals that are reasonable for yourself, and set a maximum number of excuses. I try to go to 2 - 3 events every week. One week of the month, I let myself go to just 1. This isn't practical for everyone, so set your own agenda. Just make sure you are on the winning end of the battle between goals and excuses not to meet your goals.
